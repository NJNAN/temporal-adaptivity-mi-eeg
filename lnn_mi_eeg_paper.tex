\begin{abstract}
Motor imagery (MI) electroencephalography (EEG) signals exhibit non-stationary temporal dynamics that pose challenges for conventional discrete-time neural models. Continuous-time neural networks, particularly closed-form continuous-time (CfC) models, offer a promising alternative by directly modeling continuous-time dynamics through adaptive time constants. Despite the growing interest in continuous-time models, their effectiveness on multi-class MI-EEG decoding and the interpretability of their learned temporal representations remain underexplored. In this letter, we present a systematic evaluation of CfC against LSTM and EEGNet from a continuous-time modeling perspective on the BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a. Experimental results demonstrate that CfC achieves competitive classification performance (Accuracy: X.X\%, F1: X.X) compared to LSTM and EEGNet. Through analysis of the learned time constant $\tau$ distributions, we further reveal class-discriminative temporal dynamics. Additionally, we evaluate model robustness under synthetic Gaussian noise perturbations. These findings suggest that continuous-time modeling is a promising direction for MI-EEG decoding.

\section{Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Introduction}

Motor imagery (MI) based brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) translate covert movement intentions, as reflected in electroencephalography (EEG) signals, into actionable commands \cite{wolpaw2002brain}. Unlike evoked potentials, MI-EEG signals are self-paced and non-stationary, exhibiting complex temporal dynamics such as event-related desynchronization (ERD) and synchronization (ERS) in the $\mu$ (8--13 Hz) and $\beta$ (13--30 Hz) bands \cite{pfurtscheller2001motor}. These characteristics make MI-EEG decoding a challenging task that demands models capable of capturing fine-grained temporal dependencies.

In recent years, deep learning methods have become the dominant approach for MI-EEG classification. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs), particularly EEGNet \cite{lawhern2018eegnet}, and recurrent neural networks (RNNs), primarily long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, have demonstrated strong performance on BCI competition datasets \cite{schirrmeister2017deep}. However, these models operate on discrete time steps, which may not adequately capture the continuous-time dynamics inherent in EEG signals. LSTMs, for instance, process sequential data at fixed sampling intervals and rely on gating mechanisms rather than explicit continuous-time representations, potentially limiting their ability to adapt to the non-stationary characteristics of EEG signals. This mismatch motivates the exploration of continuous-time models for EEG decoding.

Continuous-time neural networks offer a principled alternative by modeling the underlying dynamical system directly through differential equations. Liquid time-constant (LTC) networks \cite{hazir2019liquid} and their closed-form counterpart, closed-form continuous-time (CfC) networks \cite{oliveira2022closed}, parameterize the derivative of the hidden state as a function of the input, enabling adaptive time scales that can vary dynamically with the input. This property is particularly appealing for EEG signals, where the characteristic frequencies of neural oscillations differ across mental states and subjects.

Recent neuromorphic benchmark studies have begun to explore continuous-time models on motor imagery tasks. The Neuromorphic Sequential Arena benchmark \cite{chen2025neuromorphic} evaluated LTC networks on the OpenBMI dataset under a binary (left/right hand) MI setting, focusing primarily on efficiency metrics such as energy consumption, memory footprint, and training speed. While these results demonstrate the viability of continuous-time models for EEG-based MI decoding, several important questions remain unanswered. First, existing evaluations are limited to binary classification, whereas real-world BCI applications often require multi-class decoding (e.g., left hand, right hand, feet, and tongue in the standard four-class paradigm). Second, these benchmarks prioritize neuromorphic efficiency over classification performance and do not provide analysis of the learned temporal representations. Third, the robustness of continuous-time models to noise in EEG signals has not been systematically investigated.

To address these gaps, this letter presents a systematic study of CfC on multi-class MI-EEG decoding, with a focus on three aspects: classification performance, temporal interpretability, and noise robustness. Specifically, we make the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We provide a systematic evaluation of CfC, LSTM, and EEGNet on the four-class BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a from a continuous-time modeling perspective, introducing time constant $\tau$ as an analysis tool.
    \item Through visualization and analysis of $\tau$ distributions across different MI classes, we reveal that CfC captures class-discriminative temporal dynamics, providing interpretable evidence for its adaptive modeling capability.
    \item We evaluate the robustness of CfC under controlled Gaussian noise perturbations and analyze its performance degradation patterns compared to LSTM and EEGNet.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Related Work}

\subsection{MI-EEG Classification Methods}
Traditional MI-EEG classification relies on feature engineering methods, among which common spatial patterns (CSP) \cite{koles1990spatial} and its variants, such as filter bank CSP (FBCSP) \cite{ang2008filter}, have been the most widely used. These methods extract spatial filters that maximize the variance ratio between classes, followed by classifiers such as linear discriminant analysis (LDA). Despite their popularity, CSP-based methods are sensitive to non-stationarities in EEG signals and require careful subject-specific parameter tuning.

Deep learning approaches have largely superseded traditional methods in recent years. CNNs, particularly EEGNet \cite{lawhern2018eegnet}, apply spatial and temporal convolutions directly to raw EEG signals and have achieved competitive results across multiple BCI paradigms. RNNs, especially LSTM networks, model sequential dependencies through gated memory cells and have been widely adopted for MI-EEG decoding \cite{schirrmeister2017deep}. Transformer-based architectures have also been explored for MI-EEG \cite{song2022transformer}, though their high computational cost and data requirements limit practical deployment.

\subsection{Continuous-Time Neural Networks}
Continuous-time neural networks parameterize the dynamics of hidden states through ordinary differential equations (ODEs), allowing the model to adapt its time scale continuously rather than at fixed discrete steps. The liquid time-constant (LTC) network \cite{hazir2019liquid} defines the derivative of the hidden state as a nonlinear function of both the input and the current state, with a learnable time constant $\tau$ controlling the update rate. This formulation enables the network to exhibit adaptive behavior that varies with input characteristics.

The closed-form continuous-time (CfC) network \cite{oliveira2022closed} approximates the solution of the LTC ODE in closed form, achieving inference speeds one to five orders of magnitude faster than discretized ODE solvers while maintaining the core property of continuous-time dynamics. CfC has shown promising results in domains such as healthcare monitoring \cite{bhattacharjee2026adaptive} and speech processing \cite{oliveira2022closed}, but its application to motor imagery EEG remains underexplored.

\subsection{Neuromorphic Benchmark Work}
Recent efforts have evaluated continuous-time models within neuromorphic benchmarking frameworks. Chen et al. \cite{chen2025neuromorphic} introduced the Neuromorphic Sequential Arena (NSA), a comprehensive benchmark for neuromorphic temporal processing that includes LTC networks on EEG-MI tasks from the OpenBMI dataset. These evaluations, however, are primarily efficiency-oriented (energy, memory, and training speed) and employ a binary MI classification setting. In contrast to prior efficiency-oriented binary evaluations, this work investigates the more challenging four-class MI-EEG decoding task, with a focus on classification performance and temporal interpretability through $\tau$ analysis.

\section{Proposed Method}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Proposed Method}

\subsection{Closed-Form Continuous-Time Model}
The core of CfC \cite{oliveira2022closed} is the closed-form approximation of the LTC ODE. Given an input signal $\mathbf{x}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{D}$ and a hidden state $\mathbf{h}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{H}$, the LTC dynamics are defined as:
\begin{equation}
\tau \frac{d\mathbf{h}(t)}{dt} = -\mathbf{h}(t) + f(\mathbf{W}_h\mathbf{x}(t) + \mathbf{b}_h),
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the time constant, $\mathbf{W}_h$ and $\mathbf{b}_h$ are learnable parameters, and $f(\cdot)$ is a nonlinear activation. The CfC model approximates the solution of this ODE by:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}(t+\Delta t) \approx \sigma\left(\mathbf{w}_o \odot \mathbf{h}(t) + (1 - \mathbf{w}_o) \odot f(\mathbf{W}_h\mathbf{x}(t) + \mathbf{b}_h)\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{w}_o = \exp(-\Delta t / \tau)$ is a decay vector derived from the time constant $\tau$, $\Delta t$ is the time step, $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid activation, and $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication. The time constant $\tau$ is formulated as a learnable positive scalar or input-dependent function, enabling the network to dynamically adjust its temporal receptive field. A larger $\tau$ corresponds to a slower decay and a longer effective memory, while a smaller $\tau$ enables rapid updates. This mechanism is particularly suited for EEG signals, where the relevant temporal scales vary across cognitive states and artifacts.

\subsection{Baseline Models}
For a fair comparison, we adopt two established baselines. The LSTM \cite{hochreiter1997long} is the standard discrete-time recurrent baseline, using input, forget, and output gates to manage memory flow. The EEGNet \cite{lawhern2018eegnet} is a compact CNN designed specifically for EEG, using depthwise and separable convolutions to capture spatial and temporal features with few parameters. EEGNet is included as a representative compact CNN specifically designed for EEG decoding, serving as a strong spatial-temporal baseline. All models share the same input preprocessing pipeline and are trained with comparable computational budgets.

\subsection{Classification Pipeline}
The processing pipeline consists of four stages. Raw EEG signals are first band-pass filtered (8--30 Hz) to retain motor-related frequency bands. Filtered signals are then segmented into trials aligned to MI cues. Each model processes the preprocessed data to produce class predictions. Cross-entropy loss is used for training, and classification accuracy and macro-averaged F1 score are reported as evaluation metrics.

\section{Experiments}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Experiments}

\subsection{Dataset}
We evaluate on the BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a \cite{tangermann2012review}, a standard benchmark for four-class MI-EEG decoding. The dataset contains recordings from 9 subjects performing four MI tasks: left hand, right hand, feet, and tongue. Each subject performed 72 trials per class, recorded from 22 Ag/AgCl electrodes at 250 Hz. The evaluation follows a subject-dependent protocol. We follow the official evaluation protocol and report the mean and standard deviation of accuracy and F1 score across all subjects using 5-fold cross-validation.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
All models are implemented in PyTorch. EEG signals are band-pass filtered between 8 Hz and 30 Hz using a Butterworth filter (order 4). No additional artifact rejection or manual curation is applied. For CfC, the hidden dimension is set to 64 and the time constant is initialized to 1.0 and made learnable. For LSTM, a two-layer network with 64 hidden units per layer is used. EEGNet follows the architecture described in \cite{lawhern2018eegnet}. All models are trained with the Adam optimizer, initial learning rate of $1\times10^{-3}$ with cosine annealing, and a batch size of 32 for 200 epochs. Early stopping with patience of 20 epochs is applied based on validation loss. All models are trained under comparable computational budgets to ensure a fair comparison.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Discussion and Conclusion}

This letter presents a systematic evaluation of closed-form continuous-time neural networks for motor imagery EEG decoding. Our experiments on the four-class BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a demonstrate that CfC achieves competitive classification performance compared to LSTM and EEGNet, establishing continuous-time models as a viable approach for MI-EEG classification. The analysis of learned time constants reveals class-discriminative temporal dynamics, offering interpretable insights into how CfC adapts its internal representation to different motor imagery tasks.

\textbf{Limitations.} This study has several limitations. First, we evaluate on a single dataset; generalization across datasets and subjects requires further investigation. Second, the number of baseline models is limited; incorporating a broader range of methods would strengthen the comparison. Third, the noise perturbation experiments use synthetic Gaussian noise, which may not fully replicate real-world EEG artifacts such as eye blinks, muscle activity, or electrode motion. Fourth, the interpretability analysis is based on learned $\tau$ distributions and does not make direct neuroscientific claims about neural mechanisms.

\textbf{Future Work.} Future directions include (i) leave-one-subject-out evaluation to assess cross-subject generalization, (ii) validation on real-world EEG artifacts from clinical or ambulatory settings, and (iii) exploration of real-time BCI applications where the low computational cost of CfC provides a practical advantage.
