{Huang: Continuous-Time Modeling for MI-EEG Decoding}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
This letter tests a common assumption in cue-locked motor imagery EEG decoding: if temporal dynamics are a dominant source of class information, then continuous-time or selective-state-space recurrent models should materially improve discrimination and expose class-informative time scales. On BCI Competition IV-2a, we compare a CfC-style recurrent model, MI-Mamba-style selective SSM, LSTM, Tiny-Transformer, EEGNet, Shallow ConvNet, Riemann-TSLR, and spatial--spectral CfC hybrid controls under shared preprocessing, training-only standardization, matched model selection, pooled, grouped-pooled, session-wise, and leave-one-subject-out protocols. Shallow ConvNet and Riemann-TSLR are strongest in pooled and session-wise decoding, while EEGNet leads the harder unaligned leave-one-subject-out protocol. MI-Mamba-style is the strongest added temporal/SSM model in pooled and session-wise experiments, but it remains below the spatial/geometric leaders in both settings. The learned time constants $\tau$ reveal adaptive temporal regimes but not stable class-discriminative information: class distributions overlap and Friedman testing is not significant, although regenerated analyses show that $\tau$ is strongly associated with $\mu$-band power. A completed $\Delta t$/$\tau$-initialization ablation does not rescue pure CfC-style performance. Overall, within this standard cue-locked MI setting, temporal adaptivity can track sensorimotor state but does not appear to constitute a primary source of class-discriminative information; performance aligns more with spatial--spectral and geometric inductive bias than with temporal modeling capacity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{M}{otor} imagery (MI) based brain-computer interfaces translate covert movement intentions in EEG into control signals \cite{wolpaw2002brain}. Because MI-EEG is non-stationary and exhibits ERD/ERS-like temporal dynamics \cite{pfurtscheller1999erd}, it is natural to assume that stronger temporal modeling should materially improve discrimination.

Compact CNNs such as EEGNet and Shallow ConvNet remain strong MI baselines, whereas standard recurrent models often lag behind \cite{lawhern2018eegnet,schirrmeister2017deep}. Continuous-time models such as LTC and canonical CfC expose adaptive time scales directly \cite{hasani2021ltc,hasani2022cfc}, but most EEG evidence still comes from binary or efficiency-oriented settings rather than standard four-class MI benchmarks \cite{chen2025nsa}. Recent MI decoders also include FBCSP, compact temporal CNNs, selective SSMs such as MI-Mamba, convolution-transformer hybrids such as DBConformer, and graph/geometric models such as Graph-CSPNet and GAH-TNet \cite{ang2008fbcsp,ang2012fbcsp2a2b,ingolfsson2020eegtcnet,elouahidi2024simpleconv,guo2025mimamba,wang2025dbconformer,li2024ctnet,han2024eegcct,ju2022graphcspnet,han2025gahtnet}, yet these usually inject stronger spectral, spatial, or topological priors rather than isolating temporal adaptivity itself. The sharper question is therefore whether temporal adaptivity itself is a major source of discriminative power in standard MI-EEG.

We test that claim directly on IV-2a under matched preprocessing, optimization, and model selection, plus an auxiliary binary-MI check on BNCI2014-004. The main boundary is simple: sequence and selective-SSM models improve over basic recurrent baselines, but the strongest within-subject and session-wise results still come from compact spatial and geometric models, and $\tau$ does not translate into stable class discriminability under the stricter session-wise protocol.

\section{Models Under Test}

\subsection{CfC-style Unit}
The recurrent unit is a CfC-style exponential-decay update motivated by canonical CfC \cite{hasani2022cfc}. The raw EEG sample at time step $t$ is first projected to $\mathbf{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{H}$, and the hidden state $\mathbf{h}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{H}$ is updated through
\begin{align}
\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_t &= \tanh\!\left(\mathbf{W}_c[\mathbf{z}_t; \mathbf{h}_{t-1}] + \mathbf{b}_c\right), \\
\boldsymbol{\tau}_t &= \mathrm{softplus}\!\left(\mathbf{W}_{\tau,2}\,\phi\!\left(\mathbf{W}_{\tau,1}[\mathbf{z}_t; \mathbf{h}_{t-1}] + \mathbf{b}_{\tau,1}\right) + \mathbf{b}_{\tau,2}\right) + \epsilon, \\
\mathbf{h}_t &= \exp(-\Delta t / \boldsymbol{\tau}_t) \odot \mathbf{h}_{t-1}
+ \left(1 - \exp(-\Delta t / \boldsymbol{\tau}_t)\right) \odot \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_t ,
\end{align}
where $\phi(\cdot)$ is SiLU, $\epsilon = 10^{-3}$ enforces positivity, and $\boldsymbol{\tau}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{H}$ is per-unit and input-dependent. The default setting uses $\Delta t = 1.0$ and initializes the $\tau$ head near $\tau=1.0$, so the initial retention factor is $\exp(-1)\approx0.37$ per step and the contribution of a hidden state decays to below 1\% after roughly five recurrent updates when $\tau$ remains near its initialization. Thus $\tau$ should be interpreted as a relative model time scale rather than a physiological duration. We include a $\Delta t$/$\tau$-initialization ablation in the revision experiments to test whether this memory scale, rather than the inductive-bias argument, explains the CfC-style results. Layer normalization is applied to $[\mathbf{z}_t; \mathbf{h}_{t-1}]$ before a candidate head $2H \rightarrow H$ and a $\tau$ head $2H \rightarrow H \rightarrow H$. This is a practical CfC-style exponential-decay variant rather than a claim about every canonical CfC form.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare six main baselines. LSTM \cite{hochreiter1997long} is the discrete-time recurrent reference. Tiny-Transformer uses a 64-dimensional input projection, learned positional embeddings, two 4-head encoder blocks, and temporal mean-max pooling as a compact attention baseline. MI-Mamba-style is a shared-protocol selective-SSM surrogate added for the revision experiments to test whether a modern temporal state-space prior changes the conclusion. EEGNet \cite{lawhern2018eegnet} is the compact CNN baseline. Shallow ConvNet \cite{schirrmeister2017deep} serves as a stronger band-power-oriented CNN. Riemann-TSLR computes OAS covariance matrices, maps them to the Riemannian tangent space, standardizes tangent features on the training split only, and classifies them with multinomial logistic regression.

To test whether pure CfC-style dynamics mainly lack spatial inductive bias, we introduce a lightweight Hybrid-CfC-style baseline. It uses a shallow EEGNet-style frontend with 8 temporal filters, a depthwise spatial convolution across all 22 electrodes, a compact separable temporal block, average pooling, and frontend dropout 0.25, followed by a 128-dimensional CfC-style module and a linear head. The revision experiments also add SpatialSpectral-Head and SpatialSpectral-CfC controls to separate the frontend contribution from the CfC backend. These designs are diagnostic rather than full hybrid architecture searches; their purpose is to test whether a spatial--spectral frontend changes the behavior of the CfC-style recurrence, not to rule out stronger spatial--temporal hybrids.

\section{Evidence on IV-2a}

\subsection{Dataset and Setup}
We evaluate on BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a \cite{tangermann2012bci4}, which contains 9 subjects, four MI classes, 22 channels, and 250 Hz sampling. We use the standard 2--6 s post-cue window, implemented as 0--4 s relative to MI onset in MOABB. The primary pooled protocol uses trial-level 5-fold cross-validation after session pooling, with training-only normalization. To limit residual within-run similarity, we also report a grouped pooled control using session-plus-run groups in the outer split; each subject contributes 12 groups (2 sessions $\times$ 6 runs). We further report the standard session-wise split that trains on session 1 and tests on session 2.

All neural models are implemented in PyTorch; Riemann-TSLR uses pyRiemann and scikit-learn. Signals are filtered to 8--30 Hz, downsampled to 125 Hz, standardized per channel with training-only statistics, and clipped to $[-6,6]$. CfC-style uses hidden size 128 with the input-dependent $\tau$ head in (2). CfC-style, LSTM, a supplementary GRU control, Tiny-Transformer, and MI-Mamba-style all use temporal mean-max pooling or the closest architecture-specific pooling analogue. LSTM and GRU use two recurrent layers with 128 hidden units and dropout 0.4; Tiny-Transformer uses width 64, two encoder layers, four heads, and dropout 0.2. EEGNet and Shallow ConvNet follow their compact canonical settings \cite{lawhern2018eegnet,schirrmeister2017deep}. Riemann-TSLR uses OAS covariance estimation, tangent-space projection, train-only feature standardization, and multinomial logistic regression with $C \in \{0.1,1,10\}$ selected on the same validation split as the neural models. Parameter counts are 1.0k (Riemann-TSLR), 2.4k (EEGNet), 36.6k (SpatialSpectral-Head), 40.6k (Shallow), 86.8k (CfC-style), 87.0k (Hybrid-CfC-style), 101.1k (Tiny-Transformer), 125.4k (SpatialSpectral-CfC), 158.5k (GRU), 210.9k (LSTM), and 298.7k (MI-Mamba-style). Neural models use Adam, learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, cosine annealing, gradient clipping 1.0, batch size 64, and early stopping after a 25-epoch warm-up within an 80-epoch budget. We report subject-level paired $t$-tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, and Holm correction. We also run a binary-MI auxiliary check on BNCI2014-004 (train sessions 0/1/2, test sessions 3/4) and keep it supplementary to the IV-2a boundary claim.

The highest pooled accuracy is achieved by Shallow ConvNet (68.8\%, 0.686) and Riemann-TSLR (68.3\%, 0.681), whose difference is not significant in the MI-Mamba-inclusive rerun (raw $p = 0.772$). The top tier is therefore not simply ``CNN versus continuous-time'', but a compact band-power CNN plus a compact geometric baseline.

Among temporal models, MI-Mamba-style is the strongest pooled sequence/SSM baseline (59.0\%, 0.575). The grouped pooled control, which was run for the original seven-model set, preserves the same spatial/geometric top tier: Riemann-TSLR 67.8\%, Shallow 66.2\%, EEGNet 52.8\%, Hybrid-CfC-style 52.1\%, Tiny-Transformer 51.8\%, CfC-style 48.1\%, and LSTM 41.1\%. The pooled and grouped evidence therefore supports a task-level regularity: performance aligns more consistently with spatial or geometric inductive bias than with temporal adaptivity alone, suggesting that task structure, rather than model temporal expressiveness, governs performance. The same pattern argues against a simple capacity explanation: the 298.7k-parameter MI-Mamba-style model, 125.4k-parameter SpatialSpectral-CfC, 101.1k-parameter Tiny-Transformer, and 86.8k-parameter CfC-style do not exceed the 1.0k Riemann-TSLR or the 40.6k Shallow ConvNet.

Table \ref{tab:sessionwise} is the stricter primary generalization check and uses the regenerated full session-wise run after the output-integrity audit. Accuracy decreases under this harder protocol, but the ranking remains stable at the top: Riemann-TSLR and Shallow ConvNet again form the leading pair, with no significant difference between them (raw $p = 0.085$; Holm-adjusted $p = 0.594$). The leave-one-subject-out control provides a harder cross-subject stress test. In the unaligned LOSO protocol, EEGNet leads at 45.1\%, followed by Shallow ConvNet at 41.4\%, CfC-style at 39.8\%, LSTM at 39.2\%, MI-Mamba-style at 38.6\%, Tiny-Transformer at 38.0\%, and Riemann-TSLR at 34.2\%. This ranking should not be read as contradicting the within-subject and session-wise results: an alignment diagnostic shows that Riemann-TSLR rises from 34.2\% to 47.9\% when unsupervised Euclidean alignment is applied to the held-out subject, a 13.7-point improvement (paired $p = 0.001$). Thus the LOSO result mainly exposes subject-shift sensitivity and tangent-space alignment dependence, while still failing to show a temporal-model advantage.

As a limited stability sanity check, two additional clean repeats with repeat seeds 42 and 43 rerun the five-model subset under the same session-wise protocol and preserve the same ordering, with seed-level standard deviation of subject-mean accuracy below 2 percentage points for every model. A supplementary temporal-shuffle control sharpens the same point: randomizing within-trial order at test time leaves the order-invariant Riemann-TSLR unchanged, but reduces Shallow ConvNet by 27.9 percentage points, EEGNet by 13.1, CfC-style by 12.8, and LSTM by 10.7, indicating that temporal order itself is not the limiting factor for class discrimination in this task. Once session transfer is enforced, performance still tracks spatial and geometric bias much more than adaptive recurrence alone.

As a limited external-consistency check, a supplementary binary-MI experiment on BNCI2014-004 again leaves Shallow ConvNet first (80.1\%) and EEGNet second (78.3\%), while Riemann-TSLR, Tiny-Transformer, CfC-style, and LSTM cluster near 73\%. This auxiliary result does not justify expanding the main claim beyond IV-2a, but it is directionally consistent with the same boundary: compact spatial or geometric priors remain the most reliable source of performance.

\subsection{Temporal Adaptivity versus Class Discriminability}
CfC-style exposes explicit, sample-wise time constants. If temporal adaptivity itself encoded class-discriminative information, then the learned $\tau$ values should separate the four classes and align with class-dependent spectral markers under the stricter session-wise protocol. For the analysis below, we first average $\tau$ over hidden units and time steps to obtain one trial-wise value, then average within subject and class, yielding $9 \times 4 = 36$ subject-class summaries. For the spectral analysis, $\mu$- and $\beta$-band power are computed from the downsampled but otherwise unstandardized test trials using Welch power spectral density, averaged over all 22 channels within 8--13 Hz and 13--30 Hz, respectively.

Figure \ref{fig:tau} indicates broad overlap in the class-wise $\tau$ distributions. The regenerated full session-wise run gives class means of 1.565 (left hand), 1.574 (right hand), 1.631 (feet), and 1.616 (tongue). The overall Friedman test on subject-class summaries is not significant ($p = 0.706$), and no paired contrast approaches a stable class split (all raw $p \ge 0.152$). Thus the first link in the evidence chain still does not support reliable class separation by learned time scale.

The spectral associations are more nuanced after regeneration. $\tau$ correlates strongly with $\mu$-band power ($r = 0.792$, $p = 8.95 \times 10^{-9}$), but not with $\beta$-band power ($r = 0.108$, $p = 0.529$). We therefore no longer interpret $\tau$ as spectrally inert. Rather, it appears to track part of the sensorimotor $\mu$-band state while still failing to form a stable class biomarker. Time-resolved $\tau$ trajectories peak between 2.45 s and 3.13 s across classes, and early-window means (2.0--2.5 s) cluster tightly between 1.61 and 1.62. Figure \ref{fig:tau_topography} further shows that motor-adjacent channels can influence $\tau$, but this influence does not translate into consistent class discrimination. The corresponding channel table is included in the supporting materials as \texttt{revision\_tau\_occlusion\_channel\_summary.csv}. The central implication is therefore refined rather than reversed: the learned time scale is physiologically modulated, yet it is not by itself a reliable source of class-discriminative information in this setting.

The completed $\Delta t$/$\tau$-initialization ablation further rules out a trivial memory-scale explanation for the pure CfC-style result (Fig. \ref{fig:dt_tau_ablation}). Across $\Delta t \in \{0.5,1.0,2.0\}$ and $\tau_{\mathrm{init}}\in\{0.5,1.0,2.0\}$, the best pure CfC-style setting reaches 45.6\% accuracy at $\Delta t=2.0,\tau_{\mathrm{init}}=0.5$, essentially matching the regenerated default session-wise CfC-style result (45.1\%). Hybrid-CfC-style is more sensitive, with a best ablation result of 52.2\% at $\Delta t=0.5,\tau_{\mathrm{init}}=2.0$, but this remains below the Shallow ConvNet and Riemann-TSLR session-wise leaders. SpatialSpectral-CfC peaks at only 39.1\%. Thus poor default $\Delta t$ or $\tau$ initialization is not sufficient to explain the overall temporal-model gap.

\subsection{Structured Perturbations}
To move beyond iid Gaussian perturbations, we evaluate two structured distortions on the session-wise models: 8--30 Hz band-limited noise at 5 dB and random dropout of 30\% of channels. For band-limited noise, white noise is filtered to 8--30 Hz and scaled trial-wise on the pre-standardization band-passed test trials according to $\mathrm{noise\ power} = \mathrm{signal\ power}/10^{\mathrm{SNR}/10}$, after which the perturbed trials are standardized with the same training-only statistics as the clean condition. Both perturbations are repeated with five random seeds per subject and model.

The models exhibit distinct structured-perturbation profiles, but the differences are less decisive than in the clean-condition benchmark once Holm correction is applied. This is a controlled perturbation study rather than a realistic artifact simulation.

\section{Conclusion}

Our results suggest that temporal adaptivity alone is insufficient to determine the performance ceiling in MI-EEG decoding, with performance more strongly influenced by spatial, spectral, and geometric priors. Across pooled and session-wise protocols, the top accuracy is delivered by Shallow ConvNet and Riemann-TSLR; the grouped pooled control preserves the same top tier for the original seven-model set. The added MI-Mamba-style selective-SSM baseline improves the temporal-model tier and leads the revision-only grouped extension, but it remains below the spatial/geometric leaders in pooled and session-wise evaluation. The SpatialSpectral controls further show no consistent synergy from appending the current CfC backend to a spatial--spectral frontend: SpatialSpectral-CfC is below SpatialSpectral-Head in pooled, grouped, and session-wise evaluation. In unaligned LOSO, EEGNet leads and Riemann-TSLR suffers until Euclidean alignment is applied, showing that this protocol is dominated by subject-shift and alignment effects rather than by temporal adaptivity. Under the stricter session-wise protocol, $\tau$ behaves as a physiologically modulated temporal-scale indicator, including a regenerated association with $\mu$-band power, but not as a robust class biomarker. A supporting binary-MI check on BNCI2014-004 again leaves Shallow ConvNet strongest (80.1\%) and EEGNet second (78.3\%), and does not alter the main boundary claim.

A practical efficiency snapshot sharpens this tradeoff. On an RTX 4060 laptop GPU with batch size 64 and 22$\times$500 inputs, representative forward times are 1.5 ms for EEGNet, 8.0 ms for LSTM, 8.6 ms for Shallow ConvNet, 11.5 ms for Hybrid-CfC-style, 11.6 ms for MI-Mamba-style, 15.4 ms for Tiny-Transformer, and 178.0 ms for pure CfC-style; at batch size 1 the approximate per-trial latencies are 0.64 ms for Shallow ConvNet, 0.68 ms for EEGNet, 1.20 ms for Tiny-Transformer, 2.92 ms for LSTM, 11.43 ms for Hybrid-CfC-style, 11.61 ms for MI-Mamba-style, and 164.77 ms for pure CfC-style, while Riemann-TSLR requires 0.79 ms on CPU inference. These eager-mode FP32 measurements are implementation-specific snapshots, and the high CfC-style cost is dominated by the explicit per-time-step Python loop and repeated candidate/$\tau$ MLP evaluations. We did not benchmark vectorized scan, JIT, or \texttt{torch.compile} variants here, so the reported latency should be read as the cost of the present implementation rather than a lower bound for all CfC-style recurrences.

Several limitations remain. Parameter efficiency is not matched across families, $\Delta t=1.0$ means $\tau$ should be interpreted as a relative rather than physiological timescale, and the baseline set still does not include ODE/CDE models, stronger CNN/transformer hybrids such as DBConformer, or graph/deeper geometry-aware models such as Graph-CSPNet and GAH-TNet \cite{gu2023mamba,wang2025eegmamba,guo2025mimamba,wang2025dbconformer,ingolfsson2020eegtcnet,elouahidi2024simpleconv,li2024ctnet,han2024eegcct,ju2022graphcspnet,han2025gahtnet}. The revision controls now include a shared-protocol MI-Mamba-style selective-SSM surrogate, leave-one-subject-out evaluation with a Riemannian alignment diagnostic, a completed $\Delta t$/$\tau$-initialization ablation, channel-wise $\tau$ sensitivity analysis, and a stronger spatial--spectral CfC hybrid ablation.

The Hybrid-CfC-style frontend is deliberately minimal and diagnostic, and the stronger SpatialSpectral-CfC control still remains weak, so these results should not be interpreted as evidence against all spatial-continuous hybrids. We also omit cropped training, stronger shallow stems, and aggressive augmentation; these choices keep the comparison controlled, but they also mean recent within-subject reports using such enhancements can legitimately achieve higher absolute CNN accuracies than the compact reference baselines reported here \cite{ingolfsson2020eegtcnet,elouahidi2024simpleconv,li2024ctnet,han2024eegcct}. Although LOSO is now included, it remains a compact diagnostic rather than a full cross-dataset transfer study, and richer $\tau$ analyses remain open problems. We do not claim that temporal modeling is universally unimportant across EEG, only that its role is secondary in this specific standard cue-locked four-class MI setting. In asynchronous MI, continuous control, or paradigms where onset timing and state transitions are part of the target variable, temporal adaptivity may be more central than it is here. A plausible task-level reason is that standard IV-2a discrimination is anchored more strongly in spatially localized sensorimotor patterns and band-limited ERD/ERS structure, while temporal dynamics act mainly as modulation rather than the primary discriminative substrate, consistent with MOABB-scale evidence that covariance-based priors remain unusually strong across EEG benchmarks \cite{chevallier2024moabb}. Overall, within standard cue-locked four-class MI-EEG settings, this task does not appear to be fundamentally bottlenecked by temporal-scale modeling, but more strongly by spatial and spectral structure.
